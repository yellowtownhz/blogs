## Why Does It Matter
In vertical domain fine-tuning (e.g., medical/legal/code), terms like "ribonucleic acid" may be tokenized into "rib" → "on" → ... (a total of 5 tokens). The standard next-token-prediction loss fails to capture the entire word’s semantics.

## What is the Solution
During SFT, for example, when the first token “rib” is input, on top of the original next-token prediction, add **4 auxiliary heads** to simultaneously predict the next 2nd to 5th tokens. After training, discard these auxiliary heads — this brings no extra inference cost. (The auxiliary heads connect to the final layer of the original model.)

## How to Make This Idea Work

### Why is multi-token fine-tuning so hard
1. The base model is trained with standard next-token prediction. Introducing multi-token fine-tuning causes **distribution shift**, which may lead to worse performance than the base model.  
2. Auxiliary losses are generally higher than the standard next-token loss; training directly may cause the model to over-optimize for auxiliary losses. Since auxiliary heads are discarded during inference, this optimization path does not improve inference.  
3. Post-training is much shorter than pre-training, leaving limited compute to realize the benefits of multi-token fine-tuning — training finishes before its value can be fully exploited.

### Training is not as simple as it looks!
Training is done in **two stages**:  
Stage 1 — freeze model parameters and only train auxiliary heads;  
Stage 2 — freeze auxiliary heads and train model parameters.  


There are eight training details, small but important:  
1. Initialize auxiliary heads with the parameters from the model’s normal `lm_head` to ensure a good starting point.  
2. All auxiliary heads share a single unembedding layer to avoid distribution shift.  
3. In Stage 1, only the new auxiliary heads are trained; model parameters and the unembedding remain frozen. The loss function is:  $\mathcal{L}\_n = \sum_{k=2}^{n} -\alpha^{k-2} \log p_{t+k}(y_{t+k})$ with a decay factor that lowers the loss weight for auxiliary heads predicting further-away tokens to keep training stable.  
4. Training data for auxiliary heads comes from general-purpose datasets like ShareGPT and Tulu3-SFT, but responses are generated by the original model + original head, effectively distilling the output distribution into the auxiliary heads.  
5. In Stage 2, freeze both auxiliary heads and the unembedding layer. Since all heads share one unembedding matrix, updating it would affect both auxiliary and original heads, destabilizing training; hence it remains frozen throughout. Pre-trained unembedding parameters are already good enough.  
6. Stage 2 loss: $\mathcal{L}\_n = \sum_{k=1}^{n} -\alpha^{k-1} \beta \gamma \log p_{t+k}(y_{t+k})$. Here `beta=0.01` is small, and `gamma` decreases gradually over training. Early on, the model focuses more on auxiliary losses; later, it shifts toward optimizing the normal next-token loss $L_1$.  
7. Stage 1’s auxiliary head training uses general SFT data. After this, auxiliary heads may perform poorly in vertical domains (e.g., medical/code). In practice, fine-tuning the auxiliary heads for **one epoch** on domain-specific data yields a clear improvement.  
8. Profiling tricks from the author: monitor $L_1$ and $L_n$ (without coefficients). Healthy training shows:  
  a. $L_n$ decreases with more epochs, proving the model is optimizing auxiliary loss;  
  b. $L_n$ is lower than in standard fine-tuning, showing multi-token loss helps;  
  c. If $L_2 > 4.0$, auxiliary heads are unreliable — fine-tune them for one epoch on domain-specific training sets before Stage 2.

### Insights  
{{< admonition tip >}}  
Currently, Qwen3’s tokenizer performs poorly on low-resource languages — single words are often split into 2–4 tokens. This method applies equally well to such cases. Domains like geoscience with complex technical terminology could also benefit.  
{{< /admonition >}}  

{{< admonition tip >}}  
There are many tricks: two-stage training, various loss coefficient settings, parameter initialization — multi-token prediction involves substantial technical detail.  
{{< /admonition >}}  

{{< admonition tip >}}  
From the “compression is intelligence” perspective, splitting one word into multiple tokens represents insufficient compression. Multi-token prediction increases this compression. In **pre-training**, better compression implies smarter models; in **fine-tuning**, the resulting benefit is less certain.
